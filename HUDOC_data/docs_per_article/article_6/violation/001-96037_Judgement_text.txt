The case originated in an application (no. 11395/06) against the  lodged with the Court under Article 34 of the Convention for the Protection of Human Rights and Fundamental Freedoms (“the Convention”) by a Slovak national, Ms Paulína Petrincová (“the applicant”), on 14 March 2006.
The applicant was represented by M. Sobota, a lawyer practising in Nitrianske Rudno. The Slovak Government (“the Government”) were represented by their Agent, Mrs M. Pirošíková.
On 5 February 2009 the President of the Fourth Section decided to give notice of the application to the Government. It was also decided to rule on the admissibility and merits of the application at the same time (Article 29 § 3).
The applicant was born in 1919 and lives in Prievidza.
On 10 February 1992 the applicant submitted an inheritance-related claim to the State Notary in . On 21 December 1992 the applicant was informed that the ordinary courts had jurisdiction to deal with her claim.
On 11 June 1993 the applicant filed an inheritance-related claim with the Bratislava-vidiek District Court (whose case load was later transferred to the Bratislava III District Court).
On 4 October 1993 the District Court stayed the proceedings pending the outcome of related inheritance proceedings. The related proceedings were concluded on 20 November 1996.
On 19 June 2006 the Bratislava III District Court delivered a judgment. On 22 August 2006 the applicant appealed.
On 20 September 2007 the , without deciding on the merits, returned the case to the District Court to rectify errors in its judgment. On 31 October 2007 the case file was again submitted to the .
On 20 November 2008 the  upheld the first-instance judgment. The judgment was served on the applicant on 16 December 2008.
On 3 November 2005 the  found that the Bratislava III District Court had violated the applicant's right to a hearing within a reasonable time. The  awarded the equivalent of 1,794 euros (EUR) to the applicant as just satisfaction in respect of nonpecuniary damage, ordered the District Court to avoid any further delay in the proceedings and to reimburse the applicant's legal costs.
The  observed that the factual complexity of the case did not justify the unreasonable length of the proceedings. The applicant by her conduct had not contributed to the prolongation of the proceedings. The Constitutional Court further noted that during the period under consideration, which exceeded 12 years, the District Court had not proceeded with the case in an effective manner, had held no more than two hearings and delays imputable to the latter totalled more than 5 years.
